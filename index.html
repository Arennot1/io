PUBLISHED DRAFT

The Tactile Dissonance

Nov 2023 • 15 min read

Abstract

The contemporary video game industry faces a paradox: while visual fidelity and narrative complexity have increased exponentially, user engagement metrics often indicate a rise in cognitive fatigue and a decline in visceral satisfaction. This paper investigates the "Gulf of Execution" in modern gaming, hypothesizing that the transition from high-frequency, low-latency inputs (colloquially "button mashing") to animation-prioritized, high-latency interactions has severed the link of embodied cognition.

1. Introduction

Video games are unique among media in that they are experienced primarily through the hands. Unlike cinema, which appeals to the eye and ear, the video game relies on a tactile interface to bridge the gap between cognitive intent and digital reaction.

2. The Evolution of Interaction

Early controllers (NES, Sega Genesis) relied on digital switches with high actuation force. The lack of analog nuance meant that intensity was conveyed through frequency—how fast a player could press a button.

3. Ergonomics and Behavior

According to the concept of "Embodied Cognition," our cognitive processes are deeply rooted in the body's interactions with the world. When a controller removes physical resistance, the mind is free to wander.

4. Conclusion

While ergonomic advancements have saved players from RSI, they have stripped gaming of its tactile weight. The "button mash" was not just a primitive input method; it was a psychological tether.
